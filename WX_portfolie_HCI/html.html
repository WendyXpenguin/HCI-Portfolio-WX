About the Course

Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) integrated principles from computer science, psychology, sociology, design, and art to create technology centered on human experience. Throughout this course, we examined the inspiring potential and neglected issues of human-computer relationships, with a particular focus on designing for diverse users. Through historical analysis and hands-on group projects, I learned how to make a better design approach that applied human-centered thinking to resolve daily challenges.

My Design Manifesto

Five points I learned from four design sprints

Introduction

This semester in my Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) class, I didn't just learn software or theories, but I learned how to actually build things with good design principles that work for people. Over four design sprints, I participated in hands-on collaborative projects on designing websites, visualizing data, building a VR game, and making a physical stand kiosk for a store. Each project focuses on different challenges, but they are also intrinsically connected by fundamental HCI principles. Good design does not come from simply epiphany, but through a practical process of asking the right questions, brainstorming extensively, testing iteratively, taking feedback, and fixing mistakes. Here in this design manifesto I will present five points that I will carry with me from this class, with evidence from the design sprints (DS) I worked on.

Stock image: pexels.com

1. Know Exactly Who You're Building For

Every project taught me that we can't design for "everyone," a term that is too broad and vague. Instead, we need to pick a specific person and solve their specific problems. The practice of making personas helps with this, which elucidates the design process.

In DS1 (website redesign for Knotty and Board), we designed for three specific people we made up: Jimmy, 60 years old, has poor eyesight and hearing; Martha, 55 years old, wants to filter by price and hates clicking through too many pages; Amy, 61 years old, is new to online shopping and gets overwhelmed.

These vivid characters help us to better envision what we want our design to serve. Jimmy's poor eyesight meant we needed bigger text and text-to-speech functions on the smartwatch. Martha's budget concerns meant we needed an easy-to-use price filter. Amy's inexperience meant we needed to simplify the webpage and remove unnecessary or confusing parts. Every design decision came from asking: "What would Jimmy need here? Would this confuse Amy?"

DS1: design that targets our personas - clarifying stores and bigger fonts

This same idea was also emphasized in DS4 (store kiosk). Before we built anything, we recalled and shared our experiences shopping in stores. We collected our own needs for a store kiosk and referenced some existing kiosks we used, and we agreed on designing a kiosk that allows people to browse products, enjoy store services, and check out conveniently. We imagined how we want the kiosk to be if we are the customers, presenting ourselves as the specific customers who express their needs.

DS4: we referenced on the McDonald's Self Ordering Kiosk for our design (Wall Street Journal, Kevin Hagen)

In short, it is important to pick a real person with real problems and to come up with solutions for these problems. It is never a good idea to just design for the abstract "users."

2. Having Limits Actually Helps

At first, I saw limitations, such as small screens or technical constraints, as disadvantages. But I eventually learned that they actually make us more creative when developing new design ideas.

In DS1 (website redesign for Knotty and Board), the smartwatch screen was small and restrictive. We couldn't include everything from the website, so we had to prioritize: what is the most important action someone would take on a watch? We concluded that it was allowing users to quickly view product visuals and access key functions with high accessibility. Therefore, we focused on simplifying the content, highlighting product images, and integrating high-accessibility features such as text-to-speech and an AI assistant. The small screen ultimately pushed us to concentrate on what mattered most.

DS1: think about the limitation on smartwatch and turn it to be creativity

In DS2 (coffee data visualization), we initially chose to display coffee and country information on a map because it looked visually appealing and straightforward. However, the data from the archive was incomplete: many countries would appear empty and grey if we used a map. Therefore, we chose to use parallel coordinates instead, which allowed us to present the data more clearly without wasting space or providing redundant information. This limitation ultimately helped improve the clarity and effectiveness of the data communication.

DS2: our initial paper prototyping for the map visualization

In DS3 (the VR alien dog game), we didn't know how to correctly transform or position a 3D model in A-Frame. Instead of using a 3D alien dog, we used a 2D image that could bark. The barking sound actually worked well: players could locate the dog by listening, which made the search easier than relying solely on visual cues in a large, star-filled space. Our technical limitation opened up an alternative approach that ultimately created a better gameplay experience.

DS3: the 2D alien dog

In short, limitations force you to rethink what is truly necessary. When you can't do everything, you should always do the important things better.

3. Build Simple and Ugly Versions First

I used to try to make everything nearly complete from the very beginning, but I eventually realized that this strategy is highly ineffective. In fact, the faster you create a rough version, the faster you can identify what needs improvement.

In all our design sprints, we always began with paper prototyping. We sketched multiple versions, combined the best parts, and discarded the weaker ideas. This step clarified our design direction and saved us time by preventing us from diving into design tools without a clear plan.

In DS3 (the VR alien dog game), we built the game by gradually evolving from early rough versions. In version 1, our only goal was to place the planets in the scene, without any game mechanics. In version 2, we made the planets rotate and move, and added the 2D dog. In version 3, we added the barking sound and the background music while fixing remaining bugs. Each version took only a few days to develop, and the final result turned out very well. If we had attempted to build the complete game all at once, we would likely have run into many technical problems and failed to identify the features that truly worked.

DS3: start from building the basics of a solar system

In short, it's best to build the simplest functional version first: test it, learn from it, and then improve it in the next iteration. Never try to perfect everything or overload the project with content at the initial stage.

4. Listen to Feedback

People will tell you what is wrong with your design, and sometimes they even know how to fix it. Our job is to understand the issues they raise and incorporate helpful feedback into our work.

In DS1 (the website redesign for Knotty and Board), when we demonstrated our large-screen display design, people commented that the shopping cart was confusing: the cart preview and the actual cart icon were placed in different locations. They suggested connecting the two to clarify the cart function. Although this seems like an obvious issue, we didn't notice it during prototyping.

In DS2 (coffee data visualization), when we presented our chart demo, a classmate pointed out that the R-square value lacked explanation. Not everyone is familiar with this statistical term, so if we choose to display it, we should not assume universal understanding; instead, we should focus on extreme users and provide clear instructions. Based on this advice, we later added an explanatory box defining all statistical terms in our charts.

In DS4, our initial kiosk design included a "login with one-time code" page. Every classmate who tested it was confused – they didn't know what the code referred to. We quickly realized that this page was unnecessary. Moreover, during the demo day, one classmate suggested using printed furniture codes on small paper slips that customers could take and scan at the kiosk. We found this idea fascinating and immediately incorporated it into our final design.

DS4: the printed barcode from the classmates' feedback

In short, feedback often reveals issues that designers and developers themselves may overlook.

5. Design for the Real World

A design does not exist only on a computer – it exists in a physical space and is used by real people in real situations.

This became increasingly clear through the progression of my projects: DS1 and DS2 focused mainly on screen-based displays (watch, laptop, large display, and webpage); DS3 involved VR; DS4 centered around a physical kiosk in a store.

For the store kiosk in DS4, we concentrated more on the physical design than on the Figma screen layouts. We had to consider questions such as: How tall should the kiosk be for standing users? Where should the scanner be placed? How do people recognize that this kiosk is meant for shopping, and how do they know they can interact with it by touching the screen? We built the kiosk out of cardboard and placed an iPad inside to display the screens. This gave us insights more than what we could learn from digital design alone. We could stand in front of the kiosk and experience how a user would actually interact with it.

In DS3, the VR game, we also had to think about physical interactions. The initial A-Frame project ran on a computer, and the player moved using the keyboard. But because the final version was intended for mobile VR, we created a phone version where the user was always moving and would move in the direction they looked toward. We also added additional text instructions to guide the user through the experience.

DS3: the original computer-based VR that requires WASD to move

In short, design is not just about the content – it is about how people physically interact with it in the real world.

Conclusion

I learned all these valuable points by making things, watching people interact with our designs, and fixing problems along the way. Design isn't about creating something pretty – it's about creating something that truly works. Through a semester of hands-on projects, I developed a stronger design mindset, and I now feel much more prepared for future design challenges.
